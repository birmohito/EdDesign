Design Framework

Every lesson follows ADDIE

Each session in this curriculum was designed using the ADDIE model — ensuring every lesson is grounded in learner needs, clearly structured, and continuously evaluated.

A
Analyze
Identified learner age (2nd–5th grade), group size (~20), allergy restrictions, prior knowledge, and school-site constraints before designing any content.
D
Design
Mapped learning objectives to Bloom's Taxonomy levels. Structured each session in 7 phases with clear timing and purpose for each activity.
D
Develop
Created all materials: instructor scripts, ingredient shopping lists, station setup guides, and age-appropriate vocabulary for each cultural theme.
I
Implement
Led instructor-led delivery across 2nd, 4th, and 5th grade classes simultaneously, adapting in real-time to student engagement and pace.
E
Evaluate
Used Reflection & Cleanup segments as structured debrief, gathering feedback and noting areas to improve for subsequent sessions.

Design Rationale

Why these choices?

Every structural and content decision was intentional. Here's the design thinking behind this curriculum.

🌍
Cultural Sequencing
Sessions were ordered to build cultural exposure progressively — Middle Eastern → Southeast Asian → universal food safety — increasing novelty while maintaining learner confidence.
🔁
Consistent 7-Phase Structure
Every lesson uses the same structure. This reduces cognitive load, creates predictability for learners, and allows instructors to focus on content delivery rather than managing transitions.
🤝
Scaffolded Independence
Each session moves from instructor modeling → guided practice → independent application, following Vygotsky's Zone of Proximal Development. Students are never thrown in cold.
🧠
Multisensory Warm-Ups
Flavor Match and Herb Hunt activate prior knowledge through taste and smell — not just visual/verbal cues — improving retention and engagement for kinesthetic learners.
⚠️
Safety-First Allergy Design
Session 8 was redesigned to be fully dairy-free and nut-free, with allergy checks built into the welcome phase. Dietary constraints were integrated into materials lists with label-verification notes.
📊
Built-in Evaluation
Presentation & Tasting and Reflection segments function as formative assessment checkpoints, allowing real-time evaluation of learning objectives without formal testing disrupting the experience.
Cognitive Design

Bloom's Taxonomy in action

This curriculum targets multiple levels of Bloom's Taxonomy simultaneously across the 7 phases of each session.

Create  —  Groups assemble, plate, and design their own creations
Evaluate  —  Presentation discussions and peer feedback
Analyze  —  Flavor pairing decisions and technique analysis
Apply  —  Guided practice with real ingredients and tools
Understand  —  Direct instruction and demonstration
Remember  —  Warm-up recall activities
Self-Evaluation

What worked. What I'd change.

Effective instructional designers evaluate their own work with the same rigor they apply to learner outcomes.

Strengths

The 7-phase structure created strong session predictability — students knew what was coming, which reduced transition friction and increased time-on-task.
Sensory warm-up activities (Flavor Match, Herb Hunt) generated the highest student engagement and vocabulary retention across all three sessions.
Proactive allergy and dietary restriction planning in Session 8 prevented any safety incidents and built trust with school site coordinators.
Cultural context framing (naming each dish's origin and significance) increased student curiosity and cross-cultural empathy beyond the cooking skills themselves.

What I'd Improve

I would add a pre-session learner survey to capture individual dietary preferences and prior cooking experience, enabling better differentiation within each session.
The Application phase (18 mins) was tight for groups that struggled during Guided Practice. A flexible buffer of 5 minutes could be embedded for differentiated pacing.
I would develop a simple rubric for the Presentation segment so students can self-assess against clear criteria, adding a metacognitive layer to the evaluation.
Future sessions could include a visual recipe card students keep — extending learning beyond the classroom and reinforcing knowledge retention at home.
